EmployeeWorks June Release: Multi-Theme Experience, 100+ Languages and Tighter Admin Control — the GA Story Turns Deployment-Grade
NEW ServiceNow
The June 2026 Employee Slate release for ServiceNow EmployeeWorks is squarely about operationalising the platform that hit GA last fortnight: more flexibility to tailor the experience per employee, more admin visibility and control over how it runs, and global reach. The headline capability is Multi-Theme Experience — you can associate multiple conditional themes to a single Employee Slate instance and automatically serve the right branded experience to the right employees based on their context — while the underlying Moveworks assistant now supports 100+ languages. For a Deloitte HRSD practice this is the detail that makes EmployeeWorks sellable into the hardest Australian accounts: one tenant can serve a multi-agency department or a multi-brand corporate without standing up parallel instances, and 100+ languages directly addresses our multicultural workforces and APS language-access obligations. Re-price any “one-instance-per-brand” assumption in live EmployeeWorks proposals — Multi-Theme collapses that cost line.

Now Assist Moves to “Assist Packs” — Consumption-Based AI Pricing Replaces Per-Seat, and It Rewrites Every HRSD Business Case
WATCH ServiceNow Agentic
As Now Assist completes its shift from assistive GenAI to an agentic platform that can resolve cross-system work autonomously, ServiceNow’s commercial model is shifting with it: industry analysis describes “Assist Packs,” a consumption model where customers pay for AI tasks rather than just seats. That is a structural change, not a price tweak — an autonomous offboarding agent that touches dozens of systems consumes very differently from a per-seat licence. For Deloitte HRSD practitioners the actionable work is financial modelling: any TCO or business case built on seat counts needs re-basing on tasks-per-resolution, and clients will ask us to forecast and cap consumption before they sign. Get ahead of it by instrumenting a sandbox tenant now and producing a defensible tasks-per-HR-intent baseline — that is exactly what POC #2 this week builds.

Workday’s Sana Self-Service Agent Reaches General Availability Inside Microsoft 365 Copilot — the Front-Door Contest Is Now Both GA
WATCH Workday
Workday’s Sana Self-Service Agent is now generally available for eligible joint customers inside Microsoft 365 Copilot, letting employees and managers answer HR and finance questions and complete routine tasks without leaving Microsoft 365, while Workday retains security and compliance control. Read alongside EmployeeWorks GA, this is the same “HR front door moves into the collaboration layer” pattern now shipping on both the ServiceNow and Workday sides at once. For Deloitte HRSD practitioners advising the many Australian clients who run Workday for core HCM and ServiceNow for service delivery, this sharpens the live tension: two vendors now offer GA agentic front doors into the same Teams/Copilot surface. The consulting value is defining a clean division of labour — which agent owns which intent, and where the governed system of action sits — before clients accidentally fund two competing “do-the-work” layers.

General AI Hot News
Anthropic Ships — and Within 3 Days Suspends — Claude Fable 5, the First “Mythos”-Class Model, After a US Export Directive
BREAKING Anthropic
According to InfoQ, Anthropic released Claude Fable 5 on 9 June 2026 — the first publicly available model in its new “Mythos” class, built for long-horizon agentic work with always-on adaptive thinking, a 1-million-token context window and 128K output tokens — available the same day across the Claude API, AWS and Microsoft Foundry. Within three days a US government export directive temporarily forced it back offline. It is, on the reporting, Anthropic’s most capable widely-released model with state-of-the-art results across nearly all tested benchmarks. For Deloitte HRSD practitioners the lesson is governance, not benchmarks: a frontier model can vanish from supported endpoints in 72 hours for reasons that have nothing to do with the model’s quality. Any HR assistant pinned to a single frontier model needs a documented contingency and a fast failover path — treat model availability as a supply-chain risk, not a given.

Anthropic Opens Managed Agents (Public Beta) and Enterprise-Managed MCP — the Substrate for Scheduled, Governed HR Automations
NEW Agentic
Anthropic added public-beta support for Managed Agents that run on schedules and securely use CLI tools and authenticated services — cron-based automation, vault-stored environment variables and browser-capable integrations for recurring work — alongside enterprise-managed MCP connector access starting with Okta, so admins provision connectors once and users get zero-touch access on first login, with centralised authorisation across Claude chat, Claude Code and Cowork. This is precisely the plumbing that turns “an AI that answers” into “an AI that runs a job every week against your systems” (this very digest is an example). For Deloitte HRSD practitioners, the enterprise-managed MCP + Okta pattern is the answer to the IT objection that always stalls HR-AI pilots: connector sprawl and per-user authorisation. Scope HR automations around scheduled managed agents with centrally-governed connectors, and the security review gets materially easier.

OpenAI’s Deprecation Cascade: GPT-5.2 Pulled From ChatGPT (12 June), Personalisation Hits Free Tier, GPT-4.5 Retires 27 June
NEW OpenAI
OpenAI’s June is housekeeping at speed: as of 12 June the GPT-5.2 family (Instant, Thinking, Pro) is no longer available in ChatGPT, with existing conversations auto-migrated to the corresponding GPT-5.5 model; personalisation improvements rolled out to ChatGPT Go & Free on 9 June; and GPT-4.5 retires from ChatGPT on 27 June after its 30-day sunset. For Deloitte HRSD practitioners the value is in the diary, not the demo: any HR accelerator, prompt library or assistant that calls a pinned GPT model needs an audit this week and a migration before 27 June, or it silently breaks. Pair this with the Claude Fable 5 episode and the message is consistent — build HR tooling against a model abstraction, keep an eval pack to prove parity after a forced swap, and diarise every vendor cut-off.

Gemini 3.5 Flash Is Now Default and Can’t Be Disabled in Gemini Enterprise; Google Launches Managed Agents in Public Preview
HOT Google
Effective 9 June 2026 the feature toggle for Gemini 3.5 Flash is gone — it is enabled by default for all users in the Gemini Enterprise app and cannot be disabled — while gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview and gemini-3-pro-image-preview are deprecated and shut down on 25 June. Google also launched Managed Agents in the Gemini API (public preview), running autonomous, stateful agents in secure, isolated Google-hosted Linux sandboxes — the same managed-agent pattern Anthropic shipped this week. For Deloitte HRSD practitioners two things matter: the “on by default, cannot disable” change is a live model-governance flag for clients with strict model-approval controls (raise it proactively in regulated accounts), and Flash 3.5’s price/performance keeps resetting the floor for the cheap, fast routing layer behind HR chat and case triage. Refresh any cost-sensitive Now Assist alternative against it.

Qualcomm Reportedly in Early Talks to Buy Tenstorrent for US$8–10B — the RISC-V Challenge to NVIDIA Gets a Deep-Pocketed Backer
WATCH Hardware
Reporting this week has Qualcomm in early talks to acquire Tenstorrent for between US$8 billion and US$10 billion — a serious commitment to competitive AI infrastructure. Tenstorrent designs AI chips on the open RISC-V standard and carries the engineering pedigree of chip veteran Jim Keller; a deal would give Qualcomm a real seat at a hardware table currently dominated by NVIDIA and AMD. For Deloitte HRSD practitioners this is backdrop, but the right backdrop to track: more credible silicon competition pushes inference price/performance down, and cheaper inference is the single biggest lever under the economics of per-employee HR agents. Keep an inference-cost line in every HR-AI business case and revisit it as the hardware field widens — the case that does not pencil today may pencil by Q4.

AI Act Labelling Code of Practice Heads to Finalisation Around June 2026 — Employment Stays High-Risk, and Mandatory Bias Reviews Spread
Regulatory EU & US
The EU is finalising a Code of Practice for marking and labelling AI-generated content to support AI Act transparency obligations, with the work expected to land around June 2026, even as the broader “Digital Omnibus” simplification gives high-risk systems — employment use cases included — more time to comply. In parallel, the US trend is toward mandatory pre-deployment review: New York City’s education department has moved to require AI tools to pass a bias and equity review before deployment, signalling a shift from voluntary frameworks to enforced governance. For Deloitte HRSD practitioners the posture is unchanged: HR AI remains squarely in scope as high-risk, EU worker information-and-consultation duties still bite before go-live, and clients increasingly need a documented bias-and-transparency assessment in the deployment pack — bake it into the standard EmployeeWorks/Now Assist delivery checklist rather than bolting it on at audit time.
